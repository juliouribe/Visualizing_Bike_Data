Visualizing Bike Share Data in San Francisco¶

by Julio Uribe

Dataset Overview¶

This data set explores bike usage from Ford GoBike data. This data set contains over 1.8 million entries of bike usage from 2018. The data was sampled to 100,000 rows for faster pandas operations. I made use of various plot types to explore this data set. From barcharts and histograms to gps based heat maps. The next few slides are some of my favorite plots that provide the most insight on how customers use bike share to address their morning commute, how subscription models affect trip duration, and how bike usage fares throughout the week and across different parts of the year.

User Types as a Proportion¶

First, I wanted to see what pricing models customers subscribed to. Ford GoBikes offers a monthly rate, and a flat rate for single rides. Both of these come with certain rules and restrictions which can affect how riders plan their trips. Most riders opt in for a subscription model which indicates that customers are making bike share a part of their routine.

Heat Map of Trip Duration by Age¶

This plot was one of my favorites. While it wasn't as related to exploring commute related info, it produced a fairly wholesome result. My initial impression was that younger riders would be riding for longer trips. The result was that groups of all ages are riding for about the same amount of time. Sure there's more people around their early 30s making up a larger share of the trips but all groups displayed an interest in various lengths of bike rides.

Subscription Type Difference Clearly Illustrated by Trip Duration Spread¶

Pricing models can play a heavy role in how people plan their trips and bike usage. It was really interesting to see how closely subscriber users tended to end their trips just above the 10 min mark. I had trouble pulling up pricing model data from 2018 but I'm led to believe that subscribers had to limit their trips before the 15 minute mark to avoid additional fees. Meanwhile, flat rate customers enjoyed their bikes to up to 30 mins before experiencing higher fees.

Average Trip Duration Across the Week and Gender¶

The following barplot showed the average trip duration across the week with gender as color. My main take away here was that female riders tended to ride for longer on weekends. While all riders tended to limit their trips to below 15 minutes during the week, female riders didn't mind going over this limit on the weekend. Perhaps its a reflection of riding for leisure and not worring so much about the extra potential fee. What's an extra dollar or two when you're enjoying a bike ride on a beautiful day?

Morning Commute Conveyed by through Heat Maps¶

The last thing I wanted to explore was a heat map using a map of San Francisco and plotting the different docking locations. I used the library Folium to grab an image of a map from Open Street Map and then I plotted the different locations and created a heat map. The result is a story of people grabbing a bike near a public transit location and riding them to different parts of San Francisco for work, school, or other activies. The next few slides show how people move around in the morning using GoBikes.

Bike Start Locations During the Morning Commute¶

Are riders using GoBikes to connect them to public transportation or other commute options? The first heat map shows that there is a decent amount of activity spread out in San Francisco but more activity near public transit locations. There's a concentration near Bart stations, Cal Train 4th and King location, and other spots on Market. A solid highlight is the 22nd Street Cal Train Station in Dogpatch. We'll have to see where the morning trips end to see where people are going.

Closing Summary¶

I learned a lot while exploring this data set. Bike share has really increased in popularity over the last few years and is very helpful for addressing last mile problems while commuting in a city. I've personally haven't used bike share nearly as much because I own a bike myself but maybe I should consider this option more. It was interesting to see how much of an effect the different subscription models had on how riders used this service. Depending on city needs, companies can adjust their pricing models to influence how their service is used in a way that could potentially compliment the city's transit needs. Besides adressing the usual work commute, I wonder what other transportation gaps exist that bike share can address.
